# MoneyFactor Doctrine

## Affluent Household Customer Persona

BestCardsForMe is built for affluent, optimized households that want the real math behind credit card decisions. The primary user is not chasing novelty or coupon-book complexity for its own sake. They have meaningful monthly spend, strong or improving credit, regular household obligations, and enough travel, dining, grocery, business, or lifestyle activity for rewards strategy to matter.

This household is willing to pay an annual fee when the net value is clear. They do not need the cheapest card. They need the card that turns their actual behavior into durable annual value with the least unnecessary friction.

They are skeptical of issuer marketing, oversized welcome-bonus claims, and benefits that require changing behavior to justify a fee. They want a recommendation that respects their time, sophistication, and risk tolerance.

## MoneyFactor Brand Promise

MoneyFactor exists to show **The Real Math Behind Every Money Decision**.

The brand promise is to replace marketing noise with practical decision math: what something costs, what it realistically returns, where the assumptions can break, and which choice is best for the person actually making the decision.

For BestCardsForMe, that means credit card recommendations should be grounded in realistic annual value rather than issuer positioning. Annual fees, credits, perks, redemption friction, travel frequency, household spend, and preference for optimization all matter. A card is only good if it is good for the user's actual life.

## Vertical Filter

BestCardsForMe belongs to the MoneyFactor vertical of consumer finance decision engines. Every feature should pass this vertical filter:

- Does it clarify the user's economic decision?
- Does it explain net value in plain English?
- Does it reduce hype, status signaling, or issuer bias?
- Does it help an affluent household choose confidently without pretending every benefit is worth face value?
- Does it preserve trust if affiliate monetization is added later?

If a feature increases clicks but weakens decision quality, it is off-doctrine.

## BestCardsForMe Premium-Value Positioning

BestCardsForMe is not a generic no-fee card finder. It is a premium-value recommendation engine for households that can justify premium products when the math supports them.

The product should not reflexively penalize annual fees. Fees are a cost input, not a moral failure. A high-fee card can be the best recommendation when the user has:

- meaningful monthly spend
- regular travel
- lounge or premium perk usage
- willingness to optimize
- enough credit strength for premium products
- clear category alignment
- ability to use credits without manufactured behavior

At the same time, premium positioning must remain honest. If a user is simple, fee-averse, rebuilding credit, or rarely travels, no-fee and low-fee cards should remain credible recommendations.

## Premium Scoring Implications

The recommendation engine should optimize for **net annual value captured**, not lowest annual fee.

Scoring should:

- increase premium travel and perk-card weighting when travel frequency, lounge preference, spend level, optimization preference, and fee tolerance support it
- model annual fee tolerance together with perk utilization rather than scoring the fee in isolation
- reduce over-rewarding $0 annual fee cards for affluent, high-spend, optimization-friendly users
- preserve no-fee recommendations for users who explicitly want simplicity or $0 fees
- make explanations feel executive, specific, and value-oriented
- surface why popular premium cards do not fit when the value assumptions are weak

BestCardsForMe earns trust by recommending premium cards when they are justified and rejecting premium cards when they are not.
